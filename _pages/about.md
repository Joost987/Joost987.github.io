Hi! I am Joost de Nijs, a recent double Master of Science graduate from Delft University in Applied Mathematics and Applied Physics. Currently I am looking for a PhD position in Mathematics,  so if you know of an open position that aligns with my interests, please let me know. 

I have finished my Master's thesis under supervision of Bas Janssens, Yaroslav Blanter and Koenraad Schalm. It is titled: *Asymptotic symmetries of three dimensional Anti-de-Sitter space from a perspective of representation theory*. As the title mentions, it is about the asymptotic symmetries of three dimensional Anti-de-Sitter (AdS) space. These were first discovered by J.D. Brown and Marc Hennaux in 1986, in their article: [Central charges in the canonical realization of asymptotic symmetries: An example from three dimensional gravity](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/BF01211590). However, we give a slightly different definition of the asymptotic symmetry algebra, which is more oriented towards quantum mechanics in a sense. This change in definition gives you a slightly different asymptotic symmetry algebra, which is closely related to the symmetry algebra of 2D CFT's. For more details, see my [thesis](https://resolver.tudelft.nl/uuid:d195e293-afb3-47e4-b82f-12f64fb0e6c2).

Previously, I did an internship in the Heller group at Harvard University. There I worked on the quantum acoustics formalism, which aims to explain phenomena in solid state physics such as superconduction by replacing the Fock state basis with a basis of coherent states. This is quite similar to quantum optics, hence the name quantum acoustics. Specifically I developed a numerically exact method to simulate the Fröhlich Hamiltonian for extremely large bath sizes. You can find the paper explaining it under the Publications section.

For my Bachelor's thesis I worked on Modified Newtonian Dynamics (MOND). This theory aims to describe the universe without dark matter and therefore modifies Newton's law of gravity. This modification turns Newtonian gravity into a nonlinear theory and thus for example introduces an external field effect, which means that uniform gravitational fields due to external objects (such as galaxies) can change the internal dynamics of a system. This nonlinearity makes the theory difficult to simulate numerically, whereas it is extremely important to have accurate numerical simulations if you want to compare MOND to dark matter theories. In my Bachelor's thesis I developed a numerical programme, under supervision of Paul Visser and Stephan Eijt, that can carry out these simulations. You can find the code [here](https://github.com/Joost987/MONDPMesh) and my thesis [here](https://repository.tudelft.nl/record/uuid:ad94e143-0ce4-4b17-8a72-54ab9f656236).
